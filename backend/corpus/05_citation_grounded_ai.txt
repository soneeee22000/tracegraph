Title: Citation-Grounded AI: Moving Beyond Hallucination to Verifiable Truth
Domain: AI Safety, Trustworthy AI

The fundamental challenge of large language models in enterprise and regulated environments is the hallucination problem — models generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect statements with the same confidence as accurate ones. Citation-grounded AI addresses this by requiring every claim in an AI-generated response to be traceable to a specific source document, passage, or knowledge graph entity.

Three approaches to citation grounding have emerged. First, retrieval-then-cite systems (RAG with attribution) retrieve relevant passages and generate answers that reference specific chunks. Second, graph-grounded systems (GraphRAG) trace reasoning paths through knowledge graphs, providing structural evidence chains. Third, model-level citation training teaches LLMs to generate inline citations as part of their output, as demonstrated by systems like Perplexity AI and Google's research citations.

The distinction between correctness and faithfulness is critical. A response can be factually correct but unfaithfully cited — the right answer supported by the wrong evidence. Conversely, a response can be faithfully cited (accurately reflecting its sources) but factually incorrect because the sources themselves contain errors. Robust citation systems must address both dimensions.

Evaluation metrics for citation quality include Citation Recall (what fraction of claims have supporting citations), Citation Precision (what fraction of citations actually support their associated claims), and FActScore (fine-grained atomic claim verification). No single benchmark currently covers all dimensions of citation quality, representing a significant gap in the field.

For regulated industries — healthcare, legal, financial services — citation grounding is not optional. The EU AI Act requires high-risk AI systems to provide adequate traceability and documentation. The FDA's guidance on clinical decision support mandates that AI recommendations be accompanied by the evidence basis. Financial regulators expect AI-driven risk assessments to cite the data sources and models used.

The practical implementation of citation-grounded AI requires infrastructure for document provenance tracking, version-controlled knowledge bases, and user interfaces that make citation chains navigable and verifiable by human reviewers. This "human-in-the-loop" verification capability is essential for building trust in AI-generated outputs.
